Table 1: Document control summary

How to use this document
This is the complete, self-contained description of CORTEX as it will actually be built, organised
as one continuous architecture rather than a base document plus patches. If a chapter here appears
to conflict with anything a team member remembers from an earlier draft, this document wins.
Where a team member wants to add a service, database, agent, or framework not already named
in Chapter 5 through Chapter 18, the correct action is to record it in Chapter 19 and keep building
the MVP as specified here — not to build it now. Figure 5.1 on the next few pages is the single
picture that explains the whole system; every later chapter is detail on one box of that picture.

Chapter 1
Problem Overview
1.1

The problem in one paragraph

Asset-intensive industrial organisations run on documents, not databases: P&IDs, work orders,
OEM manuals, inspection reports, regulatory clauses, spare parts catalogs, and project files, each
living in its own system, each readable only by whoever already knows where to look. Engineers
spend a large share of every working day searching for information that already exists somewhere
in the organisation rather than acting on it. CORTEX exists to close that gap: it ingests the
heterogeneous document corpus of a plant, extracts and links the facts inside it into a single
knowledge graph, and exposes that graph through a cited copilot and a small set of specialist
agents, so that the answer to “why does Pump P-101A keep failing” takes seconds instead of an
afternoon of cross-referencing binders — and so that the answer to a question no single document
ever answered, like “which funnel should I use for pipe P-301”, can still be constructed, cited, and
trusted.

1.2

Root causes CORTEX addresses

• Fragmentation. A single plant operates across many disconnected document systems (P&IDs,
work orders, procedures, inspection records, spare parts catalogs), with no shared identifier
space linking a tag number in one system to the same tag in another.
• Silent knowledge loss. Experienced engineers retire carrying undocumented operational
judgement that never made it into a procedure or a manual.
• Unactionable data. Signals frequently exist (a sensor reading, a permit, a maintenance flag)
but no intelligence layer connects them to a decision before the decision window closes.
• Un-auditable answers. Even where search tools exist, they return documents, not cited,
trustable facts an engineer can act on without re-reading the source.
• Answers that were never written down. Some of the most valuable questions — “which
spare part is compatible with this pipe” — have answers that exist only as scattered specifications across several documents that were never mentioned together in any single passage.
No retrieval pathway that looks for an existing sentence will ever find them; they must be
constructed from parts.

1.3

Why a knowledge graph, not just a chatbot

A plain RAG chatbot over a document corpus answers “what does the manual say about P101A” reasonably well. It cannot answer “what else is connected to P-101A, what has failed

The one-sentence pitch
Conventional RAG retrieves a passage that already answers the question; CORTEX traverses
a graph that understands conversational context, and — when no passage ever answered the
question at all — constructs and cites an answer from the facts that do exist.

2.4

Product narrative

The product narrative that drives every UI and demo decision is graph-first: a user does not open
a document list, they open a living graph, click a node, and watch the system reveal everything
connected to it — documents, history, failure patterns, agent reasoning, even parts that were
never mentioned alongside it in any document — as a single continuous interaction rather than a
sequence of disconnected screens. Chapter 13 and Chapter 18 are built entirely in service of this
narrative; if a proposed feature does not make the graph feel more alive or more trustworthy, it
does not belong in the MVP.

2.5

Design principles

1. Cite or say nothing. No generated sentence stands without a resolvable [doc_id:passage_id]
tag or a graph fact_id; unsupported claims are rejected and regenerated, not shipped softened.
Where a constructed answer draws on general engineering reasoning rather than a document,
it is explicitly flagged as such (Chapter 11, §11.5.4).
2. Never silently overwrite. Conflicting facts both remain queryable, one marked superseded
or disputed, never deleted.
3. Retrieval follows intent, not just keywords. The system assembles context from the
current query, recent conversation, and the currently focused graph node before deciding what
to retrieve (Chapter 9).

6

CORTEX Engineering Design Document

4. Construct, don’t just retrieve. When an answer is derivable from facts that were never
stated together in any passage, the system decomposes the question into constraints, gathers
candidates, filters by compatibility, and cites every constituent fact (Chapter 11, §11.5.4) — it
does not give up because no single “golden passage” exists.
5. Fewer moving parts, more demo time. Every architectural choice in Chapter 5 trades
a small amount of theoretical scalability for a large amount of integration reliability under
a 5-week clock. Context-aware traversal and compositional reasoning are implemented as
application-level orchestration over the existing Neo4j, Qdrant, and Postgres stack — not as
new services.
6. Judge-checkable over judge-tellable. A metric the team can run live (Chapter 15) beats
a claim on a slide the team cannot defend under questioning.
7. Graceful degradation, not silent failure. Every pipeline stage (Chapter 6) has a defined
fallback path so a single bad document, a single flaky model call, or an incomplete specification
cannot take down the demo.

2.6

What “done” looks like for the MVP

The MVP is done when a single rehearsed, scripted path (Chapter 18) — click a piece of equipment,
watch its context light up via context-aware traversal, invoke CORTEX Diagnose, watch it reason
live with citations, ask CORTEX Asset a compositional “which part fits this” question and watch
it construct and cite an answer, check the compliance score, submit a correction and watch the
graph update on screen — runs reliably, end to end, against the real 100–300 document demo
corpus, in under eight minutes, with no manual intervention.

Reliability & degradation

The system has no uptime SLA (it is a single demo run), but must degrade gracefully rather than
fail visibly on stage:
• If P&ID live VLM extraction is slow or fails during the demo, fall back to 2–3 pre-processed
drawings with graph edges pre-loaded (see Chapter 17).
• If a single document fails to parse, ingestion continues for the remaining corpus; the failure is
logged, not fatal to the batch.
• If the frontier model call for Copilot generation times out, retry once with the same retrieved
context before surfacing an error state to the user.
• If an agent exceeds its max_iterations cap, it returns its best partial answer with an explicit
“inconclusive” marker rather than hanging.
• If context-aware traversal’s structural-enrichment phase (Chapter 9) is slow, it is dropped and
the answer proceeds on adaptive-traversal results alone, per the per-phase budget in Chapter 9.
• If a compositional (CGFR) query cannot find candidate specifications for one side of the comparison, CORTEX Asset returns a partial, explicitly-flagged answer rather than fabricating a
specification (Chapter 11, §11.5.4).

4.4

Explainability & trust

• Every Copilot sentence must resolve to a specific doc_id and passage_id, or, for graph-derived
synthetic passages, to a specific fact_id.
• Every fact must carry source_doc_id, confidence, and status.
• Every agent conclusion, including compositional (CGFR) recommendations, must be traceable
to the graph nodes/edges and documents that produced it (evidence chain, replayable).
• Any part of a constructed answer that is not grounded in a retrieved fact must be explicitly
labelled as general engineering reasoning, never presented as if it were sourced.

4.5

Security (demo-appropriate, not enterprise)

JWT-based auth with 3–4 hardcoded roles and route-level RBAC (Chapter 14) is sufficient. No
SSO, no ABAC policy engine, no mTLS service mesh — these are enterprise upgrade triggers, not
MVP requirements (Chapter 19).

4.6

Maintainability

• Single Python codebase for the backend (FastAPI + LangGraph + retrieval utilities); single
Next.js codebase for the frontend — two repositories, not eighteen services.
• Configuration via environment variables (Chapter 17), no hardcoded credentials or endpoints.
• Structured logging on every pipeline stage and every agent step, sufficient to debug a demo
failure within minutes, not hours.
• Context-aware traversal and compositional reasoning are implemented as pure application-level
functions and agent tools inside the existing fabric-api service — no new deployable unit is
introduced by either capability.

5.8

Request lifecycle: Copilot query

This is Figure 5.1’s middle section, spelled out step by step:
1. Client sends query + JWT + session context to POST /api/query. The payload carries
query, session_id, and an optional focused_tag — the tag of whichever graph node the user
currently has selected in the explorer (Chapter 9).
2. Role check (coarse RBAC). FastAPI dependency validates the JWT signature and expiry,
then checks the requested scope against the role claim; a failing check returns 403 with a
machine-readable reason.
3. Query classification. A single fast-tier LLM call (or a regex/keyword pre-filter with the
LLM as fallback) tags the query as factual / diagnostic / procedural / open. This tag drives
both the fusion weights (Chapter 9, Table 9.9) and the shallow-vs-full traversal depth decision.

20

CORTEX Engineering Design Document

4. Context assembly and parallel fan-out. The graph pathway first assembles a rich seed
context (current query entities, recent chat-history entities, the focused node) and then three
async retrieval calls run concurrently: the context-aware graph traversal (Chapter 9), Qdrant
vector search, and Postgres FTS/BM25 lexical search. Each has its own timeout; a slow
pathway is dropped from fusion rather than blocking the other two.
5. Merge + re-rank. Candidates from all three pathways — including the synthetic passages
produced by graph traversal — are merged, deduplicated by passage ID, and passed through
the local cross-encoder to select the top 5–8 chunks.
6. Generation + citation check. The frontier model generates a cited answer from the selected
chunks; a citation-resolution check parses every [doc_id:passage_id] tag and rejects the
answer once, regenerating with an explicit instruction to only use resolvable tags, if any tag
fails to resolve.
7. Agent trigger check. If the query or its classification matches an agent-trigger pattern
— including a component-selection or compatibility pattern that routes to CORTEX Asset’s
compositional reasoning mode (Chapter 11) — the Supervisor is invoked asynchronously and
streams a follow-up event over the same SSE channel.
8. Response + audit. The final answer and its citations are returned to the client; the query,
the resolved pathway weights, and the result are written to audit_log.

6.2

Stage 1: Document Upload

Purpose. Accept a file, register it, and hand it to the async ingestion queue without blocking
the caller. Inputs. Multipart file upload (PDF, XLSX, or image) via POST /api/documents.
Outputs. A document_registry row with status queued; a Redis/RQ job ID. Algorithm. (1)
Validate MIME type and size; (2) store the raw file to a local/mounted volume keyed by a generated doc_id (UUID); (3) insert a document_registry row (doc_id, type, authority_class,
ingested_at, status=’queued’); (4) enqueue an ingestion job referencing doc_id; (5) return
doc_id and status_url to the caller. Edge cases. Duplicate filename (allowed — doc_id is the
identity, not the filename); zero-byte upload (rejected, 422); unsupported MIME type (rejected
with a clear message naming supported types). Error handling. Storage write failures return
500 and do not enqueue a job for a file that was never durably stored. Logging. doc_id, filename,
size, MIME type, uploader (from JWT), timestamp. Expected latency. < 500ms (excludes the
async pipeline that follows). Dependencies. Postgres, Redis, local/volume storage.

Stage 2: Parsing (Docling)

Purpose. Convert a raw document into structured text with layout and bounding-box provenance. Inputs. Raw file path from Stage 1. Outputs. A structured document object: ordered elements (paragraph, heading, table cell, figure) each with page number, bounding box,
and element type; routed separately for spreadsheets. Algorithm. PDF/DOCX → Docling’s
layout+OCR+table pipeline, producing per-element bounding boxes used later for citations.
XLSX/CSV → pandas/openpyxl loader producing one structured record per sheet/row, with
a synthetic bounding box (null page, cell range as the locator) so the same citation schema
applies uniformly — this is how spare-parts-catalog rows (dimensions, material, rated pressure/temperature) become citable facts. Edge cases. Scanned pages with poor OCR confidence are still passed through (Docling’s OCR path) with a lower per-element confidence flag
propagated to Stage 3 extraction confidence. Password-protected or corrupt files fail this stage
explicitly, marking document_registry.status=’parse_failed’ rather than silently skipping.
Error handling. A parse failure on one document does not block the batch; the job is marked
failed and logged, ingestion of the remaining queue continues. Logging. Element count, page
count, OCR-confidence distribution, parse duration. Expected latency. A few seconds to ∼30s
per document depending on page count and whether OCR is invoked. Dependencies. Docling,
pandas/openpyxl. Future extension. None planned; Docling covers the MVP’s full format set

Stage 4: Entity Resolution

Purpose. Map every extracted mention to a canonical entity so “P-101A”, “Pump 101A”, and
“the feed pump” become one graph node. Inputs. Candidate triples from Stage 3; the canonical
tag list and alias table built during demo-corpus onboarding. Algorithm (2.5 stages).
1. Exact tag match against the canonical tag list.
2. Fuzzy string match (Levenshtein/Jaro–Winkler, threshold ∼ 0.85).
3. Embedding fallback (the “0.5” stage) for free-text mentions with no tag structure, reusing
the RAG embedding model already deployed.
Outputs. Resolved subject_tag/object_tag on each triple; unresolved mentions logged. Edge
cases. Two distinct canonical tags whose fuzzy-match scores tie: resolve to the higher raw stringsimilarity score and log the ambiguity. Error handling. An unresolved mention still allows the
surrounding triple’s other side (if resolved) to be written; a triple with both sides unresolved is
dropped with a log entry. Logging. Resolution stage that fired per mention, unresolved count
per document. Expected latency. Sub-second per mention for stages 1–2; embedding fallback
bounded by the existing embedding model’s call latency. Dependencies. Canonical tag list,
alias table, RAG embedding model.

node, then MERGEs the relationship edge with the fact’s confidence as an edge property. Full detail

Chunks produced during Stage 2/3 chunking (parent–child, structure-aware, capped at 512 tokens
per Chapter 9). Outputs. One Qdrant point per chunk: embedding vector + payload {doc_id,
page, bbox, authority_class}. Expected latency. Embedding call latency dominates; batched
per document.

6.8

Stage 7: Context-Aware Retrieval

Detailed fully in Chapter 9. In the data-flow view, this stage consumes the graph, the vector
index, and the lexical index built in Stages 5–6, assembles a rich seed context from the current
query, chat history, and the focused graph node, adaptively traverses the graph, and produces
a ranked, re-ranked set of 5–8 chunks for a given query. This is CORTEX’s core differentiator
versus a conventional retrieval pipeline; Table 2.2 in Chapter 2 summarises exactly what it does
that plain vector search cannot.

6.9

Consumes the re-ranked chunks from Stage 7 and generates a cited answer, as detailed in Chapter 9
and the request lifecycle in Section 5.8.

6.10

Consumes the graph (Stage 5) and, where relevant, Copilot-style retrieval (Stage 7) to produce

Full detail in Chapter 11.

6.11

Renders Copilot answers, agent outputs, compositional comparison tables, and the graph itself.
Full detail in Chapter 13.

8.2.1

Extended ontology for compositional reasoning

The relational ontology above (rows 1–7) is exactly what the system needed to answer “what’s
connected to this equipment” and “what regulation applies to it.” It is not, on its own, enough
to answer “which spare part is compatible with this equipment,” because a compatible spare part
and a piece of equipment are frequently never mentioned together in any single document — there
is no edge to traverse between them. The seven specification-type predicates in the second half
of the table above close this gap: they let CORTEX record what a piece of equipment needs
(dimension, material, temperature, pressure) and what a spare part offers (fit range, material,
rating) as separately extracted, separately cited facts, so that an agent can construct the missing
link at query time by comparing the two sets rather than requiring the link to already exist in
the graph (Chapter 11, §11.5.4).
Design decision
Extending the predicate set is a prompt-level change to the extraction LLM (Chapter 10) and
a documentation-level convention for entity_registry.attributes (Chapter 7). It requires
no Neo4j schema migration (Neo4j is schema-free on relationship types via MERGE) and no new
entity type — Specification values are stored as scalar properties on existing Equipment
and SparePart nodes, not as a new node label.

8.6

Confidence model on the graph

Every edge inherits the confidence of the fact that produced it. Text-derived facts (extraction
via rule-based, NER, or LLM methods, including specification facts pulled from spec tables and
catalogs) typically carry confidence ≥ 0.8; P&ID-derived CONNECTED_TO edges (VLM extraction)
are written at ≤ 0.6. The graph explorer visually distinguishes edges below a 0.7 threshold (dashed,
per Chapter 13) so users can see which connections are inferred versus text-confirmed. During
adaptive traversal, edge confidence directly modulates how far a relevance score propagates down
a branch (Chapter 9), so low-confidence P&ID edges naturally receive lower traversal priority
without any special-case logic.

Edge cases

9.1

Purpose and responsibilities

Purpose. Given a natural-language query, return the 5–8 passages most likely to support a
correct, citable answer, by combining three complementary retrieval pathways — graph, vector,
lexical — rather than relying on vector similarity alone, and by exploring the graph adaptively,
from a context that spans more than the current query string. This chapter is the detailed implementation of the retrieval half of Figure 5.1 (Section 5.1) and of the “Contextual Retrieval” pillar

Earlier exploratory work considered a rigid Neo4j CONNECTED_TO *1..2 traversal seeded only
by entities named in the current query. That approach produced three concrete failure modes
documented in Table 9.1 below. The fix is not a new retrieval system; it is a richer implementation of the same graph_pathway() function that already feeds the same fusion step.
Nothing about the request lifecycle (Section 5.8), the fusion weights, or the re-ranker changes
— only how the graph pathway itself decides what to explore.

9.3

Query classification

Purpose. Tag each query so pathway weights, and the traversal depth mode below, can be set
without a trained classifier. Algorithm. A keyword/regex pre-filter checks for diagnostic markers
(“why”, “keeps failing”, “root cause”), procedural markers (“how do I”, “steps to”), factual markers
(“what is”, “when was”), and compositional/component-selection markers (“which X should I use
for”, “is X compatible with”, “what fits”); ambiguous queries fall through to a single cheap LLM
classification call returning one of {factual, diagnostic, procedural, open}. A compositional marker
does not introduce a fifth query-type value; it is used purely as an agent-routing signal (Chapter 11)
— retrieval-side, a compositional question is treated as open, since it benefits from the same broad,
structurally-enriched context. Why not a learned classifier. No labelled training data and no
time to build one exist for this timeline; the rule-based router with an LLM fallback gives the
same architectural story — adaptive fusion weights — with zero ML-training dependency.

9.11

Merge and re-ranking

Algorithm. Candidates from all three pathways are deduplicated by chunk_id (graph-derived
synthetic passages get their own IDs), combined into a single candidate pool (target ∼20–30
after dedup), and passed through a local cross-encoder (bge-reranker class model, running on
CPU/GPU locally, no external API call) which scores each (query, passage) pair; the top 5–8
by cross-encoder score are selected for generation. Why local, not an LLM call. A crossencoder re-rank is cheaper, faster, and more deterministic than scoring candidates with an LLM,
and needs no extra API dependency.

Generation and citation

Prompting. The frontier-tier model is given the 5–8 selected chunks, each tagged with a stable
[doc_id:passage_id] identifier, and instructed to cite every factual sentence with exactly one
such tag, and to explicitly say “not found in the provided sources” rather than answer from

against the set of tags that were actually provided in the prompt; any tag that does not resolve
triggers exactly one regeneration attempt with an explicit reminder of the valid tag set. A second
failure surfaces the answer with the unresolved sentence flagged in the UI rather than silently
dropped. Faithfulness. Enforced by the prompt instruction above plus one self-check LLM pass
on the finished answer; sentences failing the self-check are removed before the answer is returned.
No separate NLI entailment model is hosted.

9.14

Retained fast path: shallow traversal mode

The original fixed-depth CONNECTED_TO *1..2 query is not deleted. It is kept as an explicit

implicit assumption.

Purpose. Route an incoming query or explicit invocation to exactly one worker, or to no worker

1. Explicit invocation (e.g. POST /api/agents/diagnose) routes directly to the named worker,
bypassing routing logic.

routes to CORTEX Diagnose asynchronously.
3. A query about historical patterns, forward risk, or component selection/compatibility/specification
comparison routes to CORTEX Asset — the same worker handles all four, distinguished
only by which internal tools and prompt branch it invokes (§11.5).
4. A query naming a regulation or asking about compliance status routes to CORTEX Comply.
5. No match: Copilot answers directly, no worker invoked.

11.5.1

Four capabilities, one worker

CORTEX Asset supports four distinct question shapes without becoming four different agents:
1. Normal QA / history. “What has failed on P-101A before?” — the original failure-history
and risk-scoring behaviour.
2. Recommendation generation. “What should we watch for on P-101A?” — the forwardframed narration of the same failure-history query.
3. Compatibility analysis and specification comparison. “Is F-42 compatible with P-301?”
/ “Compare F-42 and F-44 for pipe P-301.” — structured multi-attribute comparison via the
tools in §11.5.3.
4. Compositional reasoning / component selection. “Which funnel should I use for pipe
P-301?” — the full Constraint-Gather-Filter-Reason chain, §11.5.4.
All four share the same LangGraph node, the same tool registry, and the same max_iterations
cap; only the prompt branch and the subset of tools invoked differ, selected by the Supervisor’s
routing signal and, within CORTEX Asset itself, by a lightweight intent check on the query.

11.5.2

Prompt strategy for capabilities 1–2 (QA/history)

Given a tag, the worker’s LangGraph node runs the Cypher failure-history query (Chapter 8),
then prompts the frontier model to narrate the result twice: once framed forward (“based on
this pattern, what should we watch for”) and once backward (“here is what has happened before on similar equipment”). Tool calling. graph_query(tag) (the enhanced, context-aware
context_graph_query described in Chapter 9); max_iterations=3. Failure recovery. If the
graph query returns no history (a genuinely new asset), the worker returns an explicit “no history
available” response rather than fabricating a pattern.

11.5.4

Compositional Reasoning: the Constraint-Gather-Filter-Reason (CGFR)
pattern

Why this exists
Some of the most valuable industrial questions have answers that were never written down
in any single document. “Which funnel should I use for pipe P-301?” is never answered by
a sentence like “use funnel F-42 for pipe P-301” anywhere in the corpus — that sentence
does not exist. The answer is only derivable from specification facts scattered across multiple
documents (an OEM manual, a P&ID drawing, a process data sheet, a spare parts catalog).

54

CORTEX Engineering Design Document

No embedding search, no BM25 match, and no graph traversal — however context-aware —
will find a single passage that states the answer, because there is no edge in the graph between
P-301 and any funnel; they were never mentioned together. The system must construct the
answer from parts. This is fundamentally a reasoning workflow, not a retrieval pathway,
and it runs strictly after evidence retrieval: the flow for these queries is User Query →
Query Classification → Retrieval → Evidence Fusion → Reasoning, with CGFR as
one reasoning strategy CORTEX Asset selects only for this query class.

CORTEX Diagnose

Decision flow (plan-then-execute).
1. Plan. Given the query and the target equipment tag, generate a short plan: which graph
neighbourhoods and document types to pull as context.
2. Execute. Pull context-aware equipment context (adaptive traversal, Chapter 9, invoked in
“deep” mode) plus retrieved passages for the plan’s targets.
3. Hypothesize. Generate ranked causal hypotheses, each with an evidence citation.
4. Self-critique. A second frontier-tier pass reviews each hypothesis against its cited evidence
and discards any hypothesis not actually supported.

56

CORTEX Engineering Design Document

5. Stream. Each step is streamed as an SSE event and logged with a sequence number, enabling
Replay Reasoning (Chapter 13).
Tool calling. context_graph_query(tag, query, depth="deep"), retrieve(query); max_iterations=5.
Failure recovery. If self-critique discards every hypothesis, the worker returns an explicit “insufficient evidence for a confident root cause” response.

11.7

CORTEX Comply

Decision flow. For each of the curated 10–15 regulation clauses relevant to the equipment
scope: run the compliance gap Cypher query (Chapter 8); for each gap, resolve the specific
missing evidence type; aggregate into a single score and a red/amber/green list. Tool calling.
context_graph_query(reg_code, depth="shallow"); max_iterations=3. Failure recovery.
A regulation with no equipment in scope is skipped from the score denominator, not counted as

State management

Session/auth state (JWT, role) lives in a top-level context provider. Graph state
(currently centred node, expanded depth, highlighted paths, and the currently focused
tag ) lives in a dedicated GraphStore so both the explorer and the side panel can react
to the same selection without prop drilling, and so focused_tag can be read by the
query client on every Copilot request. Streaming state (in-flight tokens, citations,
agent steps, including CGFR’s four-step chain) is buffered in a per-session reducer
keyed by session_id/job_id, appended to as SSE/WS events arrive, and flushed to the
UI on each animation frame. No browser storage (localStorage/sessionStorage) is used;
everything lives in memory for the session.

13.5

Streaming UI

Copilot tokens render incrementally as they arrive over SSE; each citation event attaches
a small citation chip to the sentence being generated, clickable to open the source
passage with its bounding box highlighted. Agent reasoning events (plan, hypothesis,
critique, and –- for CORTEX Asset’s compositional mode –- constraint, candidate, compatibility
recommendation) render as a vertically stacked timeline in the Reasoning Overlay, each
entry linking back to a highlighted graph node or document.

13.6

Graph animations

Node selection triggers a Cytoscape.js layout re-centre and a staged reveal: connected
documents and sister equipment fade in over ∼400ms, followed by the maintenance timeline
sliding in from the side panel, followed by any active failure-pattern edges pulsing
to draw attention. Edges below a 0.7 confidence threshold (Chapter 8) render dashed.

the streaming overlay already produces.

ComparisonTable component used for both a standalone compare_specs call and the final
step of a full CGFR chain.

Sequencing if the project continues past the hackathon

Should CORTEX continue past the hackathon, the recommended order of investment follows
the trigger conditions above, roughly: (1) ingestion volume growth triggers the Kafka
backbone before anything else, since it is purely additive to the existing pipeline
shape; (2) a second real customer triggers Keycloak/OPA before a bitemporal store,
since access control is usually the first procurement blocker; (3) a dedicated material-compat
lookup tool and a selectionRule relationship type are the next-cheapest wins, since
they directly reduce the residual hallucination risk in compositional reasoning identified
in Chapter 10; (4) graph scale, full GDS, and the P&ID vision pipeline are the most
expensive upgrades and should wait for clear evidence (node counts, drawing-topology
precision requirements) rather than being pre-built speculatively.

19.4

Closing

Every cut recorded in this document removes something a real production platform would
eventually need and nothing a hackathon judge can actually evaluate in a live demo.
What remains is still a genuinely differentiated product: a knowledge graph built
from real industrial documents, hybrid retrieval with honest citations, a context-aware
graph traversal pipeline that understands conversational intent rather than just keyword-match
entities, a working multi-agent system with three specialist workers that map directly
onto the problem statement’s own suggested features, a compositional reasoning capability
that constructs answers no document ever stated, a P&ID-to-graph pipeline, and an evaluation
suite the team can defend under questioning because every number in it was actually
measured on the actual demo corpus.
